The true cost of timesheets detached from drawings.

The expensive part is not collecting hours. It is rebuilding the relationship between time, drawing progress, manager decisions, leave, budgets, and the plan your team actually ran.

TL;DR

Engineering teams lose control when hours are captured in one place, drawing progress in another, leave somewhere else, and approvals in a manager's memory. Generic time tracking records effort. Drawing-led time tracking connects effort to deliverables.

The problem is not the timesheet form

Most drafting and engineering teams already have some way to record time. That is not the failure point. The failure point is context. A line that says "7.5 hours on Project A" does not tell a manager which drawing moved, which stage changed, whether the work matched the morning plan, whether the person was available, or whether the hours should be approved.

That gap is why weekly review meetings become archaeology. Someone opens a project tracker, a drawing register, a spreadsheet, chat notes, leave records, and the timesheet export, then tries to rebuild the day from fragments.

The costly timesheet is the one that looks complete but cannot answer what deliverable changed.

Why generic time tracking under-serves engineering teams

Generic time tracking tools are usually built around people, tasks, or clock events. Engineering delivery is built around deliverables. The unit of control is often a drawing number, document package, revision stage, or client issue milestone.

That is why the useful question is not "who worked eight hours?" The useful questions are more specific:

  • Which drawing or document asset received the time?
  • What stage was it in before and after the work?
  • Was the work part of today's plan?
  • Did the delivered note match the expected outcome?
  • Was the person available, or was leave/holiday context missed?
  • What changed when a manager approved, edited, or rejected the entry?

What drawing-led time tracking changes

Advanced Mechanix Timesheets treats hours as part of the delivery record. Staff log time against projects, drawings, activity types, descriptions, and dates. Drawing and document assets carry stages, completion percentages, comments, deadlines, and history. Managers approve with context, not from memory.

The product joins four records that engineering teams usually keep separate:

  1. The planner: what the team intended to do today, this week, and for each project.
  2. The timesheet: what work was recorded, by whom, against which project and deliverable.
  3. The register: what stage each drawing or document reached.
  4. The management layer: approvals, edits, late reasons, exports, analytics, leave, and availability.

Verification is useful only when it stays secondary

Some projects need stronger proof of hours. For those cases, consent-based screenshot and activity evidence can help. But monitoring is not the product. If the core workflow is weak, screenshots only create a larger pile of disconnected evidence.

The operating layer matters more: morning planning, drawing-level hours, stage progress, manager decisions, leave context, analytics, and exports.

Signs your team needs a drawing-led system

  1. Timesheets are approved before the drawing register is checked.
  2. Morning meetings start by asking what happened yesterday.
  3. Budget conversations happen after the overrun is already visible in invoices.
  4. Leave and public holidays are discovered after work was assigned.
  5. Client billing questions require reconstructing chat history.
  6. Managers cannot compare planned work, delivered notes, and actual hours in one view.

Two or more of those signals means the issue is not effort capture. It is operating context.

Frequently asked questions

Why is generic time tracking weak for engineering teams?
Generic time tracking records hours, but engineering delivery also depends on drawing numbers, document stages, progress percentages, approvals, leave, budgets, and exportable evidence. If those records are separate, managers have to rebuild the story manually.
What does drawing-led time tracking add?
It attaches time to the actual deliverable: project, drawing or document asset, activity, description, date, stage, completion percentage, approval state, comments, and history.
Does software remove manager approval?
No. The manager still approves. The product gives managers better context: planned work, delivered notes, drawing progress, late reasons, edits, and history.
Is optional verification the main point?
No. Optional consent-based verification can support selected cases, but the core product is planning, deliverables, timesheets, approvals, leave, analytics, and exports.
