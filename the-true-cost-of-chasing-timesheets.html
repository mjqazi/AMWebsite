The true cost of chasing timesheets.

Late, wrong, and missing timesheets look like an annoyance. Priced out across a year, they behave like a hidden department — one that produces nothing.

TL;DR

For engineering and contracting firms, timesheet chasing costs far more than the admin salary attached to it. The real losses hide in payroll correction cycles, under-billed T&M hours, and job-cost data that arrives too late to steer a project. Software helps capture; it doesn't process. Somebody has to own the weekly grind — and it doesn't have to be your project team.

The problem nobody budgets for

Every engineering or contracting business over about 20 field staff runs an informal timesheet department. It doesn't appear on the org chart. It's assembled every week from fragments of other people's jobs: a payroll administrator merging spreadsheets, supervisors texting crews on Sunday night, project managers deciphering handwriting on a photographed paper sheet, an accountant reversing last cycle's mistakes.

Because the cost is smeared across a dozen roles, nobody sees a line item for it. And because payroll always ships eventually, the process is declared "fine." It's the same pattern we see with drafting backlogs: the work gets done, so the bleeding is invisible — until you price it.

Where the money actually goes

Timesheet cost lands in four buckets. Most firms only ever count the first one.

Cost bucketWhat it looks likeWho pays it
Admin processingCollecting, deciphering, keying, coding, and correcting entries every cyclePayroll / office staff
Chase timeSupervisors and PMs pursuing missing or ambiguous entries instead of running workYour most expensive people
Payroll errorsOverpayments rarely returned, underpayments always corrected, off-cycle runs, morale damageFinance — and crew trust
Billing leakageT&M hours recalled from memory days later are systematically understated and miscodedRevenue, silently

A worked example

Take a contractor with 60 field technicians, one payroll administrator, and six supervisors. Assume conservative figures — adjust them to your own payroll and see where you land:

  • Processing: the administrator spends roughly half their week on timesheets. At a loaded cost of $65,000/year, that's ~$32,000.
  • Chase time: each supervisor loses two hours a week to chasing and clarifying entries. Six supervisors × 2 h × 48 weeks at a loaded $75/h is ~$43,000.
  • Error corrections: a 3% entry error rate on 60 weekly timesheets means ~94 corrections a year. At an hour of combined admin, supervisor, and finance time each, call it $9,000–$12,000 — before counting any overpayment that's never recovered.
  • Billing leakage: if even 1% of billable field hours are lost to late, vague, or miscoded entries, 60 techs × 1,800 field hours × 1% × a $95 charge-out rate is over $100,000 in revenue that was earned but never invoiced.

That's roughly $180,000–$190,000 a year for a mid-sized crew — and the biggest bucket, leakage, never shows up in any report because the hours simply don't exist on paper. The exact numbers will differ at your firm; the shape of the problem won't.

The most expensive timesheet is the accurate-looking one. A missing sheet gets chased. A plausible-but-wrong one flows straight through to payroll, job costing, and the client invoice.

Why engineering timesheets are uniquely painful

Timesheets at a design office or contracting firm are not the timesheets of a retail roster. Complexity compounds:

  • Cost codes. Hours don't just need a total; they need allocation across projects, phases, and activity codes — often hundreds of live codes at once. Every miscode corrupts job costing and WIP reporting downstream.
  • Overtime and allowance rules. Daily and weekly overtime thresholds, shift loadings, travel time, per-diems, site allowances — frequently varying by state, award, or union agreement.
  • Certified payroll and prevailing wage. On US public works, Davis-Bacon-style certified payroll makes a timesheet error a compliance exposure, not just an accounting one.
  • Job costing feeds decisions. Labor is usually the largest controllable cost on a project. If coded hours land two weeks late, your earned-value and burn-rate views are fiction precisely when a project starts drifting.
  • Field reality. Crews finish at 6 p.m., tired, often without connectivity. Asking them to be meticulous data-entry clerks is a losing strategy; the process has to absorb imperfect input.

Why software alone doesn't fix it

Most firms have already bought an app — sometimes two or three. Capture genuinely improves: photos of paper sheets give way to mobile entry, maybe geofenced clock-ins. Then the projects team discovers what payroll always knew: capture was never the hard part. Processing is.

Somebody still has to, every single week:

  1. Chase the entries that didn't arrive by cutoff.
  2. Resolve exceptions — 14-hour days, overlapping jobs, unrecognized cost codes.
  3. Validate hours against schedules, site diaries, or clock-in data.
  4. Apply the overtime, allowance, and award rules the app wasn't configured for.
  5. Produce the payroll file, and answer every query it generates.
  6. Turn coded hours into labor-cost reports a PM can act on.

If no one owns that list, the software simply digitizes the same errors faster. This is the "garbage in" problem we flag in industrial data analytics engagements too: dashboards built on unvalidated inputs give you confident-looking wrong answers.

What a managed timesheet desk actually does

This is the model behind our timesheet management service. The offshore production team that already runs overnight drafting cycles applies the same working pattern to time data:

  • Daily collection and digitization — paper photos, app exports, text messages, whatever your crews actually produce today.
  • Validation against rosters, schedules, and clock-in data; exceptions flagged the same day, not at cutoff.
  • Cost-coding to your live job and phase codes, with unrecognized codes queried rather than guessed.
  • Exception chasing with supervisors before the payroll deadline — the desk does the nagging, not your PMs.
  • Payroll-ready export in your payroll system's import format, on your cutoff schedule.
  • Weekly labor-cost reports per project, so job costing reflects last week — not last month.

Because the desk works while North America and Australia sleep, Friday's timesheets are processed, validated, and coded before Monday's coffee — the same overnight rhythm our drafting clients already rely on.

When outsourcing is not the answer

Honesty clause: below roughly 20 field staff, with a single pay agreement and no job-costing requirement, a decent app plus your office manager is the right answer, and we'll tell you so. Outsourced processing earns its keep when headcount, cost-code volume, or compliance rules turn timesheets into a genuine weekly workload — or when the person currently doing it is a supervisor or PM whose time is worth multiples of an admin's.

Signs it's time to take timesheets off your team's desk

  1. Payroll prep regularly consumes more than a day per cycle.
  2. Supervisors spend Sunday nights collecting hours by text.
  3. Off-cycle correction runs happen more than once a quarter.
  4. Job-cost reports lag the work by two weeks or more.
  5. T&M invoices are assembled from memory and generosity.
  6. One person holds the whole process in their head — and they take vacations.

Two or more of these, and the informal timesheet department is already costing you more than a formal one would. Frequently asked questions

How much does manual timesheet processing cost an engineering firm?
For a typical contractor with 50 to 150 field staff, the visible cost is one to two payroll administrators plus two to four hours per supervisor per week chasing and correcting entries. The larger cost is usually invisible: payroll correction cycles, under-billed time-and-materials hours, and job-cost reports that arrive too late to act on.
Does timesheet software solve the problem on its own?
Rarely. Software fixes capture and storage, but somebody still has to chase missing entries, resolve exceptions, verify cost codes against active jobs, apply overtime and per-diem rules, and prepare the payroll file. If nobody owns that processing work, the software simply digitizes the same errors faster.
What does an outsourced timesheet desk do?
A managed timesheet desk collects and digitizes entries daily, validates hours against schedules and job codes, chases exceptions with supervisors before the payroll cutoff, applies overtime and allowance rules, and delivers a payroll-ready export plus weekly labor-cost reports per project.
When should a firm not outsource timesheet processing?
If you have fewer than about 20 field staff, a single pay agreement, and no job costing requirement, a good app plus an office manager is usually enough. Outsourcing pays off when headcount, cost codes, or compliance rules make processing a real weekly workload.
