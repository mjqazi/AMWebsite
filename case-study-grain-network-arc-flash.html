38 sites, one harvest window, zero interruptions.

How an Australian grain-handling network refreshed its arc flash studies across 38 sites in nine months — without stopping a single receival site.

Project Snapshot

Client: Australian grain-handling network (anonymized composite — identifying details altered; representative of the engagement). Scope: IEEE 1584-2018 arc flash studies across 38 receival and port sites, ~520 buses. Platform: ETAP. Duration: 9 months, staged around harvest. Field data collected by the client's electricians using our structured collection packs.

The situation

The client operates a network of grain receival sites and port terminals spread across a very large area — each with its own transformers, MCCs, and distribution boards driving conveyors, elevators, aeration fans, and ship loaders. Their last network-wide arc flash assessment predated both the IEEE 1584-2018 method and several rounds of site upgrades. An insurer review flagged the gap; internally, the electrical team already knew the labels on the gear no longer matched the systems behind them.

Three constraints shaped the engagement:

  • The harvest window is untouchable. During intake season the sites run hard, and nobody is walking a consultant through a switchroom.
  • Documentation quality varied wildly by site. Some sites had current single-lines; others had drawings two upgrade cycles old, or none that could be located.
  • Onshore study quotes didn't scale. Priced site-by-site by local consultancies, the program's cost was roughly double the approved budget — a familiar shape of problem (see our outsourcing pricing benchmarks).

What we did

The engagement ran as a staged program rather than 38 separate studies:

  1. Site triage. All 38 sites ranked by documentation quality, electrical complexity, and operational criticality. Port terminals and the largest receival sites first.
  2. Structured data collection packs. Rather than flying engineers between remote sites, we built per-site collection packs — photo checklists, nameplate forms, settings capture sheets — that the client's own electricians completed during routine site visits. Our team validated each pack overnight and returned queries within 24 hours.
  3. SLD verification and redraw. Every site got a verified single-line as a study prerequisite; 14 sites needed substantial redraws from field data. These became client-standard drawings, not just study inputs.
  4. ETAP modeling and studies. Short-circuit, protection coordination review, and IEEE 1584-2018 incident-energy calculations at every relevant bus, including reduced-arcing-current cases and per-bus electrode configurations.
  5. Labels and remediation register. Print-ready labels per site, plus a network-wide remediation register: recommended protection-setting changes ranked by risk reduction per dollar — most requiring only settings work by site electricians, no hardware.

Deliverables

  • 38 verified site single-line diagrams (14 fully redrawn)
  • Per-site ETAP models, handed over in native format
  • Short-circuit & coordination study reports per site
  • IEEE 1584-2018 incident-energy results for ~520 buses
  • Print-ready arc flash labels for every labeled equipment item
  • Network remediation register: recommended setting changes, ranked
  • Program summary for the insurer and the board HSE committee

Results

The program finished inside the originally approved budget that onshore quotes had doubled. After the client's electricians implemented the first tranche of recommended setting changes, the number of buses with calculated incident energy above 8 cal/cm² at the working distance fell by roughly three-quarters — the few that remain are queued behind switchgear upgrades already on the capital plan. Most durably, every site now has a verified single-line and a native ETAP model, so the five-year review cycle becomes an update, not another archaeology project.
