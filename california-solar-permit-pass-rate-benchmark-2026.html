California solar permit pass-rate benchmark.

How EPC teams should measure first-submission quality, correction-cycle causes, and AHJ learning across commercial rooftop solar packages.

Benchmark definition

Permit pass rate should mean more than "eventually approved." Track first-submission acceptance, correction-cycle count, cause category, AHJ-specific notes, and days lost to preventable documentation errors.

The metric that matters

For a commercial solar EPC, a permit package that needs two avoidable correction rounds is not a paperwork annoyance. It moves installation labor, procurement, utility coordination, and customer expectations. The cleanest benchmark is first-submission pass rate: the percentage of packages accepted without documentation-driven correction.

Some AHJ comments are unavoidable because reviewers interpret local requirements differently. The controllable benchmark is whether the package had preventable issues: missing labels, inconsistent equipment references, incorrect sheet notes, outdated code language, structural attachments not coordinated, or utility forms that do not match the drawing set.

Common correction-cycle causes

  • Equipment schedules and single-line diagrams disagree after a late substitution.
  • Roof layout, attachment schedule, and structural notes do not tell the same story.
  • Code notes are copied from a previous AHJ without local cleanup.
  • Utility interconnection forms lag the electrical drawing revision.
  • Rapid shutdown, labeling, placard, or disconnect notes are incomplete.

How to improve pass rate

The highest-performing permit teams do not rely on memory. They maintain an AHJ-specific checklist, close each reviewer comment into a cause category, and update the package template before the next submission. This is exactly how a permit operation becomes faster over time instead of merely busier.
