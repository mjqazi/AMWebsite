How to brief an offshore drafting team.

Ninety percent of "offshore quality problems" are briefing problems wearing a disguise. Here is the pack that gets the first issue back right.

TL;DR

Send a golden reference drawing, your CAD standards, the code and jurisdiction, a complete input inventory, a scope statement with exclusions, naming rules, a revision protocol, and written acceptance criteria. Run a 45-minute kickoff, batch RFIs daily, keep a decision log — and pilot with one drawing before releasing the package.

Why most briefs fail

When an outsourced drawing comes back wrong, the instinct is to blame the vendor's competence. Sometimes that's fair — we wrote a whole guide to vendor evaluation about it. But after years of onboarding engagements that previous vendors fumbled, we can report the less comfortable pattern: most first-package failures trace to information that never left the client's office.

Your in-house drafter carries ten years of tribal knowledge: which client hates leader lines crossing, that "Panel DB-3" on level 2 is actually fed from the mezzanine, that your Microstation levels don't match the documented standard because of a 2019 migration nobody wrote down. None of that travels by osmosis. It has to be packed, deliberately, once — then it's reusable on every project after.

The eight-part briefing pack

1. The golden reference drawing

One recent drawing — ideally two or three — that you consider done right. Approved, issued, representative of the discipline you're outsourcing. This single artifact transfers more unwritten convention than any standards document: annotation density, dimensioning habits, how you handle continuation references, what your title block looks like when filled in properly. If you take nothing else from this article: never brief a drafting team without one.

2. CAD standards, as they actually are

Title block template, border files, layer/level naming, block or cell libraries, text styles, dimension styles, plot styles or pen tables. Two rules:

  • Send the files, not screenshots or PDFs of them. A .dwt/.dgnlib/.rte seed file eliminates a whole category of setup drift.
  • If your documented standard has diverged from practice (it usually has), say so. "Follow the golden drawing where it conflicts with the standards PDF" is a perfectly good instruction — and an honest one.

3. Codes and jurisdiction

Name the code edition, not just the code: NEC 2023, AS/NZS 3000:2018, CSA C22.1:24. Name the jurisdiction and any local amendments. If drawings will face an AHJ or a client review body, say which one — reviewers have habits, and an experienced offshore team will draft to them.

4. Source input inventory

List every input you're supplying and its trust level: marked-up PDFs, site photos, field redlines, the previous revision's native files, vendor cut sheets, points lists. Flag which inputs are authoritative and which are "best available." The most damaging brief we see is a folder dump with no map — the team either drowns in it or, worse, picks the wrong file as truth.

5. Scope statement with explicit exclusions

Three sentences of inclusion, then the exclusions — which matter more. "Update SLD and panel schedules to redlines. Cable schedule NOT in scope. Do not renumber existing circuits." Exclusions prevent the eager-vendor failure mode: work you didn't ask for, billed or not, creating review load either way.

6. Naming and numbering rules

Drawing numbers, sheet naming, file naming, revision codes (P1/A/0? A/B/C then 0? IFC vs AFC?). If you follow ISO 19650 or a client-mandated convention, send the container-naming spec. Renaming forty files after delivery is exactly the kind of non-work outsourcing was supposed to remove.

7. Revision and transmittal protocol

How work comes back to you: transmittal format, native files or PDF-only per issue, who receives what, what a revision cloud should capture, how superseded files are handled. Agree on it before drawing one, not during issue one.

8. Acceptance criteria

Write down what "done" means: passes your title-block completeness check, zero unpurged xrefs, plot-ready at named scales, matches golden-drawing annotation style, code references present in notes. A vendor with a real QA process (ask to see the checklist — we publish the gates in our vendor checklist) will fold your criteria straight into it.

The kickoff call: 45 minutes, one agenda

The pack transfers the documents; the call transfers judgment. Insist that the vendor's lead engineer for your account attends — not just an account manager. Agenda that works:

  1. Walk the golden drawing (10 min). You narrate what makes it right.
  2. Walk one input (10 min). Take the messiest redline and interpret it together.
  3. Scope and exclusions read-back (5 min). The vendor restates scope in their own words. Misunderstandings surface here, cheaply.
  4. Logistics (10 min). RFI channel, batching time, transmittal format, revision codes.
  5. First deliverable and date (5 min). One drawing, small, fast.

The read-back is the highest-value five minutes of the engagement. "So we are updating the SLD and schedules to Rev C redlines, no renumbering, cable schedule untouched" — said back to you — catches more errors than any QA gate.

Running the RFI loop

Questions are a good sign — a team that asks nothing in week one is drafting on assumptions. But an unmanaged question stream burns your day. Three rules:

  • Batch daily. One consolidated RFI list at an agreed time, not a message drip. The timezone gap works for you: questions land at your morning, answers reach the team by their morning.
  • Commit to your own SLA. In practice, offshore production stalls waiting on client answers far more often than clients wait on drawings. A 24-hour answer commitment on your side protects your own schedule.
  • Keep a decision log. A shared sheet: question, answer, date, decided-by. It stops re-asking, survives staff changes on both sides, and becomes project documentation for free.

Pilot with one drawing

Never release a 40-sheet package to an unproven workflow. Send one representative drawing — not the simplest one — through the full loop: brief, draft, QA, transmittal, your review. Every friction you find on one sheet you would otherwise have found on forty. This is precisely why our standing offer is the first drawing free: the pilot is the only test that reveals actual production quality, so it shouldn't cost you anything.

Five briefing failure modes to avoid

  1. The folder dump. 3 GB of unlabeled inputs and "it's all in there." It isn't.
  2. The verbal brief. A one-hour call with no pack behind it. Everything decays within a week.
  3. The perfection stall. Waiting to outsource until standards are perfectly documented. Send the golden drawing and iterate.
  4. The silent revision. Changing scope mid-package without re-issuing the scope statement. The team drafts to the old one.
  5. The proxy briefer. Briefing through someone who doesn't own the technical outcome. Judgment doesn't survive the relay.

Brief well once, and the pack becomes an asset: every subsequent project starts from a proven baseline, and your offshore team compounds knowledge of your standards the way an in-house hire would — whether that's electrical drafting, BIM modeling, or MEP documentation.

Frequently asked questions

What should a brief for an offshore drafting team include?
Eight things: a golden reference drawing, your CAD standards (title block, layers or levels, blocks or cells, fonts, plot styles), the applicable codes and jurisdiction, an inventory of source inputs, a scope statement with explicit exclusions, drawing naming and numbering rules, the revision and transmittal protocol, and written acceptance criteria.
How long should briefing an offshore drafting vendor take?
Assembling the pack takes half a day to a day the first time, then minutes on every later project because it is reusable. The kickoff call itself needs 30 to 45 minutes. That investment typically saves multiple revision rounds on the first package.
What is the biggest cause of offshore drafting rework?
Unstated assumptions. Tribal knowledge that never leaves your office — preferred layouts, unwritten annotation habits, client-specific quirks — cannot be drafted to. A golden reference drawing transfers most of it in one file.
How should RFIs be handled with an offshore team?
Batch questions once per day rather than as a stream, commit to a response SLA on your side (offshore teams stall waiting on client answers more often than the reverse), and keep a shared decision log so answered questions never get re-asked.
