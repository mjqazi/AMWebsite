The timesheet service we built for ourselves.

Before we offered timesheet management to anyone, we had to run it — every day, across two continents, for our own production team. This is that story.

Operations Snapshot

Team: Advanced Mechanix's own production group — engineers and drafters in Pakistan, front office in San Diego. Challenge: track, validate, and bill every production hour across dozens of concurrent client engagements, three pricing models, and multiple time zones. This page describes our internal operation — the process now offered to clients as the timesheet service.

Why we had to get this right first

An outsourced engineering firm has exactly one product underneath all the drawings: trust. Our clients receive invoices from a production team they have never sat next to, in a time zone they sleep through. If those invoices were ever vague — hours that didn't reconcile, line items that couldn't be traced — the entire model would collapse, no matter how good the drafting was.

Our billing structure makes the problem harder, not easier. We sell three engagement models side by side — fixed-scope projects, per-drawing capacity extension, and monthly managed-service retainers. Each demands a different kind of time truth:

  • Fixed-scope projects need accurate effort data so the next quote is priced from evidence, not optimism. Our published cost benchmarks are only honest because the underlying hours are.
  • Per-drawing work needs hours mapped to individual deliverables, so we know which drawing types are profitable and which rate-card lines need revisiting.
  • Retainers need utilization reporting — clients paying for guaranteed capacity deserve to see how that capacity was spent.

So the discipline was never optional. Timesheets are the load-bearing wall of the business.

The desk we run

The internal process, as it operates today:

  1. Daily logging against project codes. Every production engineer logs time daily — not weekly — against client, project, and deliverable codes. A drawing number is a cost code. Nobody reconstructs a week from memory on Friday.
  2. Overnight validation. An operations coordinator reviews the previous day's entries every morning: unrecognized codes queried, improbable hours flagged, gaps chased while the memory is a day old, not a week old. It is the same overnight rhythm our drafting clients see, pointed inward.
  3. Cross-checks against production records. Because we run formal revision logs and transmittals on every engagement, logged hours are reconciled against what actually moved: drawings issued, revisions closed, QA gates passed. Time claims and document control confirm each other.
  4. Weekly utilization and burn reports. Every Monday, engagement leads see the week's hours per project against budget and retainer capacity — early enough to steer, not autopsy.
  5. Traceable invoicing. Every invoice line traces to validated, coded entries. When a client queries a line item, the answer is a lookup, not an investigation.

The practical payoffs, stated plainly rather than dressed up as statistics: payroll and billing preparation is a review task, not a reconstruction project. Invoice disputes are rare because every line is traceable to a validated entry — and when a query does come, it's answered from the record, same day. Quotes improve every quarter because they're built on real effort data. And when a project starts consuming more hours than it should, the Monday report says so while there's still time to act.

From back office to service

The push to productize came from clients, not from a strategy session. Engineering managers who saw our transmittal-grade invoices asked what tooling we used; contractors juggling paper timesheets from field crews asked whether our back office could absorb theirs. The honest answer was that the "tooling" is mostly discipline — daily capture, overnight validation, coded everything, reports that arrive while they're still useful — and that discipline transfers.

That is what the timesheet management service is: the desk we already run, offered outward. Same overnight processing window, same validation habits, same traceability standard — applied to your crews' hours instead of ours. What it costs firms to not run this way is quantified in the true cost of chasing timesheets.
