The operating layer that became Advanced Mechanix Timesheets.

Before it became a product, the system was an internal discipline: connect every production hour to the drawing, stage, plan, approval, and client-ready record behind it.

Operations Snapshot

Team: Advanced Mechanix's own distributed engineering operation, with a San Diego front office and production staff in Pakistan. Challenge: keep time, drawings, stages, availability, approvals, and client evidence aligned across concurrent engagements. Outcome: the operating model became Advanced Mechanix Timesheets.

Why a normal timesheet was never enough

Advanced Mechanix sells engineering deliverables. That means an hour is only useful when it can be tied back to what changed: a single-line diagram, a BIM package, a permit drawing, a QA pass, a revision closeout, or a client issue milestone.

When the team was small, that connection could live in people's heads. As projects, clients, and time zones multiplied, memory was no longer a control system. The business needed a record that joined the morning plan, the drawing register, the timesheet, leave context, and manager approvals.

InputProjects, drawings, stages, activity types, budgets, people, and availability.
Operating layerDaily plan, drawing-level hours, delivered notes, approvals, edits, and late reasons.
OutputStage progress, project views, analytics, exports, billing evidence, and review history.

The internal workflow

The process that shaped the product is straightforward:

  1. Plan the day around deliverables. Work is assigned by project and drawing or document package, not just by person.
  2. Log time against the actual work. Production staff record hours against projects, drawings, activities, descriptions, and dates.
  3. Track stage progress beside hours. Drawing and document assets move through configurable stages with completion percentages, comments, deadlines, and status history.
  4. Approve with context. Managers review entries with planned work, delivered notes, drawing movement, late reasons, and edits visible.
  5. Export evidence. Analytics and exports support project reviews, billing conversations, and future estimates.

What changed once the workflow was productized

Productizing the process forced the internal habits into explicit software concepts: role-aware views for employees and managers, configurable stages, leave and public holidays in the same workflow, project budgets and cost views, Excel exports, and tenant-aware company setup.

It also clarified what the product is not. Optional consent-based screenshot and activity evidence can support selected verified-hour situations, but monitoring is not the product. The product is the operating layer that connects planning, deliverables, time, progress, approvals, leave, analytics, and exports.

Why the origin matters to buyers

Advanced Mechanix Timesheets was not invented for a generic office workflow. It came from the practical constraints of running drafting and engineering work across clients, drawings, stages, time zones, and managers who need evidence before they approve hours.

That is why the product language is specific: drawings, document assets, configurable stages, percent complete, morning planner, late reasons, role-aware approvals, budgets, hours, costs, leave, public holidays, and exports. Those are the records engineering managers actually need to run delivery.
