12 floors, two risers, one coordinated package.

A commercial tower needed main and emergency risers, per-floor panel schedules, UPS integration, and generator coordination without slowing the BIM coordination cycle.

Project Snapshot

Client: US commercial building team, anonymized. Scope: electrical riser diagrams, per-floor panel schedules, UPS and rooftop generator coordination, lighting panel distribution, BIM 360 coordination support, and NFPA 70E inputs.

The coordination problem

The project had already entered cross-discipline BIM coordination when the electrical team hit a documentation bottleneck. Architecture and structural changes had moved shafts and equipment rooms, while emergency power assumptions were still being resolved. The internal team could either slow coordination to rebuild the electrical package or keep coordination moving and risk a drawing set that no longer matched the model.

Advanced Mechanix was brought in to produce the riser and panel package while the client's engineers kept ownership of design decisions and code review.

What changed

  • Main and emergency risers were rebuilt from the latest model exports and equipment schedules.
  • Per-floor panel schedules were normalized so naming, feeder references, and loads matched across sheets.
  • UPS, generator, and life-safety loads were separated clearly in the riser hierarchy.
  • Three coordination rounds were processed with explicit redline logs instead of informal markup drift.

Result

60%Faster vs. baseline
24Drawings issued
3Clash rounds

The package moved through coordination without forcing the electrical engineering team to pause other design work. The client received editable source files, PDF issue sets, and a clean reference structure for final NFPA 70E and construction documentation.

Why it worked

The engagement stayed useful because drafting production and engineering decisions were separated. Our team absorbed the documentation volume; the client's engineers retained the decisions that required local judgment. That division kept the schedule tight without turning drafting into uncontrolled outsourcing.
